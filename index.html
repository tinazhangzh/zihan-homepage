About Me

I am a graduate student in Statistics at Columbia University, with a dual undergraduate degree in Statistics and Mathematics from UNC Chapel Hill. My academic work lies at the intersection of causal inference, quantitative methodology, and applications to health and social inequality.

I am particularly motivated by real-world problems where data are observational, incomplete, or drawn from multiple sources. My work integrates causal inference, machine learning, and Bayesian tools to estimate treatment effects, model policy impacts over time, and address heterogeneity across populations.

I see statistical science not only as a powerful toolkit for discovery, but also as a principled way to reason under uncertainty and make transparent, data-driven decisions. As I prepare to pursue a PhD in Statistics, Quantitative Social Science, or Public Health, I am especially excited to contribute to research that advances methodology while remaining grounded in meaningful application—whether in policy, behavioral science, or public health.

Independent Research Projects

📊 Diabetes and Cognitive Decline (Ongoing)

Evaluating the causal effect of time-varying diabetes exposure on cognitive outcomes in older adults.

This project focuses on the Health and Retirement Study (HRS) to investigate how diabetes influences cognitive decline over time. By harmonizing longitudinal exposures, outcomes, and confounders, the study applies both parametric and non-parametric causal estimators to address dynamic treatment effects and population heterogeneity.

🎓 How Gender Affects Professors’ Compensation

Explored salary disparities between two genders using causal inference frameworks in U.S. academia.

This project investigates gender-based salary disparities among university faculty using causal inference techniques (both parametric and non-parametric methods). By using public salary data and academic productivity indicators, the study estimates the gender wage gap while adjusting for institutional, departmental, and career-related confounders.

📚 Citation Networks

Investigated scale-free citation networks and preferential attachment models through Probability and Machine Learning areas.

This project explores the structure and evolution of citation networks using models of preferential attachment. It reveals how academic influence accumulates over time and highlights structural inequalities in citation-based visibility.

Research Assistant Works

👶 Baby’s First Years (BFY)

Analyzed panel data to examine how early income supplements impact child development.

This project uses data from the Baby’s First Years study to evaluate how early economic interventions influence children's cognitive and behavioral outcomes. It operates longitudinal RCT data to assess policy impact on early development.

🧬 CINner Tumor Evolution Modeling

Simulated chromosomal instability and clonal evolution to study how mitotic division errors and whole-genome duplication shape tumor progression.

This project uses the CINner framework to model how diverse mitotic division types (bipolar, tripolar, tetrapolar) and genome duplication events contribute to clonal diversity, chromosomal instability, and evolutionary selection in tumors.

🧓 HCAP/ADAMS Harmonization

Harmonized cognitive health measures across aging-focused datasets to study cognitive decline.

This work focuses on aligning and integrating data from HCAP and ADAMS to enable longitudinal tracking of aging and cognitive outcomes. It supports robust causal analysis of dementia-related trajectories.

🧠 Causal Inference Literature

Simulated high-dimensional methods and evaluated their statistical performance.

This project surveys and tests high-dimensional causal inference methods, such as doubly robust estimators and machine learning approaches. Simulations explore robustness across settings with complex confounding structures.
