About Me

I am a graduate student in Statistics at Columbia University, with a dual undergraduate degree in Statistics & Mathematics from UNC Chapel Hill. My academic work lies at the intersection of causal inference, quantitative methodology, and computational statistics, with particular focus on applied questions in the social sciences and health policy.

I am deeply motivated by problems where careful design and statistical rigor are essential but where real-world data are messy, incomplete, or observational in nature. My research draws from causal inference, machine learning, and Bayesian methods to address complex challenges like estimating treatment effects, modeling policy impact over time, and integrating heterogeneous data sources.

I see statistical science not only as a powerful toolkit for discovery, but also as a principled way to reason under uncertainty and make transparent, data-driven decisions. As I prepare to pursue a PhD in statistics or quantitative methods, I am especially excited to contribute to research that advances methodology while remaining grounded in meaningful application‚Äîwhether in policy, behavioral science, or public health.
